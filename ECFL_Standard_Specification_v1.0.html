Prefatory Statement

Executive Summary

The European Common Framework for Financial Literacy (ECFL) is a standardised, hierarchical competence framework that defines what it means to be financially literate — from elementary awareness to advanced mastery — in six progressive bands across 100 discrete levels.

The ECFL was developed in response to a documented and persistent financial literacy deficit across Europe. According to the Standard & Poor's Global Financial Literacy Survey (2014), fewer than 52% of adults in the European Union demonstrate basic financial literacy, with significant variation across Member States. The OECD/PISA 2021 Financial Literacy assessment shows that a substantial proportion of 15-year-olds lack the skills required to make even basic financial decisions. Existing standards — including the EU/OECD Financial Competence Framework (2022), PISA assessments, and professional certification regimes such as the CFA or EFPA designations — serve important but partial purposes. None provides a universal, continuous scale from novice to authority that can be applied across educational, professional, and regulatory contexts simultaneously.

The ECFL fills this gap by borrowing the structural logic of the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR), which has proven successful as a pan-European standard for language proficiency across 184 countries. Like the CEFR, the ECFL uses a hierarchical band system with plain-language can-do statements that describe observable competences rather than abstract knowledge requirements. Each of the six ECFL bands — F1 through F6 — corresponds to a distinct profile of financial capability, from elementary awareness (F1) to thought leadership and institutional authority (F6). Within each band, a continuous 100-level scale enables fine-grained measurement of progress.

The ECFL in brief: Six bands (F1–F6), 100 levels, four EU/OECD competence areas, six certifications, designed for universal adoption across education, employment, and regulation.

The ECFL defines performance standards across four EU/OECD competence areas: (i) Money and Transactions; (ii) Planning and Managing Personal Finances; (iii) Risk and Reward; and (iv) Financial Landscape. Can-do statements anchor each band to observable, assessable outcomes. Band certification examinations (F1–F6) are specified in full, including question counts, difficulty calibration, competence area distribution, pass scores, question pool requirements, retake policies, and certificate validity periods.

The ECFL is designed to be implementation-agnostic: it describes what competence looks like at each level, not how instruction should be delivered. Educational providers, assessment bodies, employers, and regulators are each provided with dedicated implementation guidance in Section 5 of this document.

The ECFL is published and maintained by Quadratic OÜ (Tallinn, Estonia) with an independent Stakeholder Advisory Board drawn from academic institutions, financial industry bodies, and regulatory authorities. The framework is subject to a triennial review cycle to ensure continued alignment with evolving EU financial competence policy and international best practice.

Section 1

Background and Rationale

1.1   The Financial Literacy Gap in Europe

Financial literacy — defined broadly as the combination of awareness, knowledge, skill, attitude, and behaviour necessary to make sound financial decisions and achieve individual financial well-being — is widely recognised as a critical life skill and a component of economic resilience. Yet decades of measurement have consistently demonstrated substantial deficits in financial literacy across European populations.

The Standard & Poor's Global Financial Literacy Survey, which assessed 150,000 adults across 144 countries, found that fewer than 52% of EU adults could be classified as financially literate. The OECD/PISA 2021 Financial Literacy assessment, covering 15-year-olds across 20 countries, found that approximately one-fifth of students could not perform tasks beyond the simplest financial literacy level. The OECD/INFE Adult Financial Competency Survey (2022) shows persistent gaps in areas including investment knowledge, tax literacy, retirement planning, and protection from financial fraud.

These gaps carry material consequences: over-indebtedness, inadequate retirement savings, susceptibility to scams, and sub-optimal insurance and investment decisions impose economic costs measured in hundreds of billions of euros annually across the EU, as documented in European Banking Authority and European Commission reports on retail investor behaviour.

Despite clear policy consensus on the importance of financial literacy — articulated in the EU Retail Investment Strategy (2023), the Council Recommendation on Financial Competences in Education and Training (2024), and numerous national financial literacy strategies — Europe lacks a common language for describing, measuring, and certifying financial competence.

Existing frameworks are valuable but partial. The EU/OECD Framework provides conceptual architecture without a certification mechanism. None provides a continuous, universal scale that can be used to describe a 14-year-old school student, a first-generation retail investor, a bank branch employee, and a senior portfolio manager on the same reference system.

1.3   Methodological Foundation: the CEFR Analogy

The structural logic of the ECFL is modelled on the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR), published by the Council of Europe in 2001 (updated 2018). The CEFR has achieved near-universal adoption — used in 184 countries by more than 120 million language learners and cited in national law, employment contracts, academic admission criteria, and immigration policy across Europe. Its success derives from three design principles that the ECFL replicates:

  1. Hierarchical banding with can-do statements: Competence is described in terms of what a person can do in real-world situations, not in terms of abstract knowledge. This makes the framework actionable for teachers, learners, employers, and policymakers simultaneously.
  2. Granularity within bands: The CEFR provides six bands (A1–C2) but also sub-bands and a continuous scale, enabling fine-grained assessment and curriculum design. The ECFL extends this principle to 100 discrete levels within six bands.
  3. Domain-neutral architecture: The CEFR defines what proficiency looks like without prescribing how instruction should be delivered. The ECFL adopts the same principle: it defines financial competence outcomes without mandating curriculum, pedagogy, or platform.

The analogy is not merely structural: financial literacy and language proficiency share the property of being continuous, multidimensional competences that develop gradually and are exercised in context-dependent ways. Both domains benefit from a common reference system that enables portability of credentials across institutions and jurisdictions.

1.4   ECFL Governance and Maintenance

The ECFL is published and maintained by Quadratic OÜ, a financial technology company incorporated in Estonia (EU). Governance of the framework is supported by an independent Stakeholder Advisory Board comprising representatives from academic financial literacy research, financial industry associations, regulatory authorities, and educational assessment bodies. The Advisory Board reviews proposed revisions to the framework and advises on alignment with evolving EU policy.

The ECFL is subject to a triennial review cycle. Revisions are numbered by version (e.g., Version 1.0, Version 2.0) and date. Minor clarifications may be issued as point releases (e.g., Version 1.1). All versions are archived and publicly accessible. Assessment bodies and educational institutions are expected to migrate to new versions within 18 months of publication.

Framework Structure: the Six Bands

2.1   Overview

The ECFL organises financial competence into six progressive bands, each corresponding to a qualitatively distinct profile of financial capability. The bands are designated F1 through F6 and labelled as follows:

Within each band, levels progress from foundational acquisition of band competences (lower levels) to secure mastery at the band ceiling (upper levels). Progression from one band to the next represents a qualitative shift in the nature and complexity of financial tasks the individual can perform, not merely a quantitative accumulation of knowledge.

2.2   Band Descriptors and Can-Do Statements

The following subsections provide the full can-do descriptor for each band. Descriptors are organised by the four EU/OECD competence areas: (i) Money and Transactions; (ii) Planning and Managing; (iii) Risk and Reward; and (iv) Financial Landscape.

F1 — Financial Awareness    (Levels 1–10)

Equivalent: Pre-literate adult; CEFR A1–A2 | PISA Level 1–2 | Below EFC

Global descriptor: The F1 candidate is aware that money, banking systems, and financial products exist. They can perform simple transactions under instruction, understand the difference between income and expenditure, and recognise that financial decisions carry consequences. They require guidance for all but the most routine financial tasks.

  • Money & Transactions: Can identify common forms of money (cash, debit, bank transfer); can open and use a basic bank account; understands that purchases cost money and that accounts can become overdrawn.
  • Planning & Managing: Can distinguish between needs and wants; can track monthly income and basic expenses; understands the concept of saving and can set a simple savings goal.
  • Risk & Reward: Understands that financial products carry varying risk; recognises that higher returns are generally associated with higher risk; can identify common financial scams and frauds.
  • Financial Landscape: Knows that banks and financial institutions exist; understands that governments regulate financial services; is aware that financial crises and market fluctuations occur.

Certification: F1 — Financial Awareness Certificate (lifetime validity).

F2 — Financial Foundation    (Levels 11–25)

Equivalent: Financially literate adult; CEFR B1 | PISA Level 3–4 | Approaches EFC

Global descriptor: The F2 candidate can independently manage everyday personal finances. They understand the mechanics of banking, credit, basic investments, and insurance. They can compare financial products, build a budget, and make informed decisions about saving and borrowing. They are aware of financial regulation and consumer rights at a functional level.

  • Money & Transactions: Can manage multiple bank accounts, use digital payment systems, understand credit cards and interest, and identify consumer protection rights; can compare bank products on key metrics (interest rate, fees, access).
  • Planning & Managing: Can construct a household budget across income and expense categories; can calculate compound interest; understands mortgage mechanics, rental costs, and insurance premiums; can identify tax obligations at a basic level.
  • Risk & Reward: Understands volatility and diversification; can distinguish between asset classes (equities, bonds, cash, property); understands the concept of a mutual fund and passive investing; can assess risk tolerance in basic terms.
  • Financial Landscape: Understands the roles of central banks, commercial banks, insurance companies, and investment firms; is aware of interest rate cycles and their effect on savings and borrowing costs; can interpret basic financial news.

Certification: F2 — Financial Foundation Certificate (5-year renewal or lifetime at issuer discretion).

F3 — Financial Proficiency    (Levels 26–45)

Equivalent: Informed investor / junior professional; CEFR B2 | PISA Level 5–6 | ≈ EFC

Global descriptor: The F3 candidate can apply financial knowledge to complex personal and entry-level professional scenarios. They can analyse financial statements, construct diversified investment portfolios, interpret macroeconomic signals, understand derivatives at a foundational level, and engage with tax and estate planning. They can advise on personal finance with confidence.

  • Money & Transactions: Can handle international payments, foreign exchange, and structured product mechanics; understands clearing and settlement; can identify MiFID II consumer rights and protections.
  • Planning & Managing: Can construct a multi-goal financial plan integrating savings, investment, debt management, tax optimisation, and insurance; understands pension structures (defined benefit, defined contribution, individual pension products); can apply basic estate planning concepts.
  • Risk & Reward: Can apply Modern Portfolio Theory to construct a diversified portfolio; understands duration, convexity, and yield curve dynamics; can evaluate equity valuations using P/E, EV/EBITDA, and DCF; understands options payoff profiles and basic hedging strategies.
  • Financial Landscape: Can analyse monetary policy decisions and their transmission mechanisms; understands credit cycles and sovereign risk; can assess the regulatory environment for retail investors; identifies key macro risk factors (inflation, unemployment, trade, geopolitical).

Certification: F3 — Financial Proficiency Certificate (3-year renewal).

F4 — Financial Expertise    (Levels 46–65)

Equivalent: Financial professional; CEFR C1 | ≈ EFA / CFA Level I

Global descriptor: The F4 candidate operates at a professional standard. They can model, analyse, and advise on complex financial structures. They have mastery of institutional-grade risk tools, advanced derivatives pricing, regulatory frameworks, and multi-asset portfolio management. They can serve as investment advisers, analysts, compliance officers, or product specialists.

  • Money & Transactions: Understands derivative transaction mechanics (OTC and exchange-traded), settlement risk, and clearing systems; can advise on complex payment structures including cross-currency and structured settlements.
  • Planning & Managing: Can construct and manage multi-asset wealth management plans for high-net-worth individuals; applies tax planning, succession structures, and corporate finance at a professional level; understands business valuation and M&A mechanics.
  • Risk & Reward: Can calculate and interpret Value-at-Risk (VaR), Expected Shortfall, and factor risk decomposition; prices options using Black-Scholes and the Greeks; constructs and stress-tests institutional portfolios; understands tail risk and correlation breakdown under stress.
  • Financial Landscape: Applies MiFID II, prudential capital requirements, and market conduct rules at an operational level; understands supervisory expectations for systemic risk; can assess regulatory impact of new financial products and structures.

Certification: F4 — Financial Expertise Certificate (3-year renewal).

F5 — Financial Mastery    (Levels 66–85)

Equivalent: Senior practitioner; ≈ EFP / CFA Level II–III

Global descriptor: The F5 candidate demonstrates mastery of advanced financial concepts. They can design sophisticated investment strategies, develop quantitative models, advise on complex multi-jurisdictional wealth structures, and lead institutional investment functions. They exercise independent research judgment, identify novel market opportunities, and manage systemic exposures.

  • Money & Transactions: Understands payment system architecture and settlement risk at a structural level; designs treasury operations and derivatives trading strategies for institutional counterparties; is aware of emerging payment technologies and their regulatory implications.
  • Planning & Managing: Designs and implements sophisticated multi-generational wealth structures including trusts, foundations, and cross-border succession plans; manages complex corporate and institutional financial mandates; leads teams of financial professionals.
  • Risk & Reward: Develops advanced quantitative risk models; designs and implements factor-based investment strategies; manages multi-asset portfolios under complex mandate constraints; advises on systemic financial risk and macro-financial stability.
  • Financial Landscape: Contributes to policy discourse on financial regulation and market structure; designs institutional strategies for changing regulatory environments; advises regulators and policymakers on financial stability, capital adequacy, and consumer protection.

Certification: F5 — Financial Mastery Certificate (3-year renewal).

F6 — Financial Authority    (Levels 86–100)

Equivalent: Thought leader; above CFA Level III; senior regulator / academic / CIO

Global descriptor: The F6 candidate has made original contributions to financial knowledge, practice, or policy. They can design financial standards and frameworks, shape regulatory policy, innovate financial products and markets, and lead the profession at the highest level. F6 is not merely a measure of accumulated expertise but of demonstrated intellectual leadership and impact.

  • Money & Transactions: Can design and critically evaluate payment system architecture; contributes to policy on monetary policy transmission, digital currencies, and financial infrastructure; anticipates and manages technology-driven disruption.
  • Planning & Managing: Shapes institutional financial planning frameworks; leads financial architecture design for large-scale institutional mandates; contributes to long-term financial policy and standard-setting.
  • Risk & Reward: Advances risk management science and practice; contributes to financial innovation and theoretical development; designs frontier financial instruments; shapes academic and professional discourse on market structure and risk.
  • Financial Landscape: Shapes financial policy, regulatory frameworks, and systemic risk standards; contributes to international standard-setting bodies; leads academic or professional programmes that advance the field.

Certification: F6 — Financial Authority Certificate (5-year renewal; portfolio-based assessment).

Section 3

Assessment Methodology

3.1   Two-Layer Assessment Model

The ECFL uses a two-layer assessment model. The first layer is continuous formative assessment: ongoing measurement of progress within a band, tracking performance across the four competence areas and 100 levels via knowledge checks, practical exercises, and adaptive quizzes. This layer is the responsibility of educational providers and platforms implementing the ECFL and is not directly governed by this specification.

The second layer is summative band certification: formal, standardised examinations conducted or accredited by ECFL-approved assessment bodies, which confer externally recognised band certifications (F1–F6). This specification governs band certifications exclusively. Certification exams are the authoritative, portable credential recognised by employers, regulators, and academic institutions.

The two layers are complementary: continuous assessment supports learning progression, while band certification provides external validation at the band ceiling. Certification is not required to progress through levels within a band; it is a formal endpoint credential that attests to competence at or above the band standard.

3.2   Band Certification Examinations (F1–F6)

The following specifications define the authoritative requirements for each band certification exam. Assessment bodies must comply with these specifications to issue ECFL-recognised certifications. Deviations require prior written approval from Quadratic OÜ.

F1 Certification — Financial Awareness Certificate

Official Title: Financial Awareness Certificate (ECFL F1)

Eligibility: Minimum Level 8; no prerequisites. Open to all ages 14 and above.

Time limit: 45 minutes. Attempts allowed: Unlimited; 24-hour cool-down between attempts.

Format: 30 multiple-choice questions. One correct answer per question; four options. Questions test recognition and basic comprehension. No scenario questions.

Competence area distribution: Money & Transactions 35% · Planning & Managing 35% · Risk & Reward 20% · Financial Landscape 10%.

Difficulty calibration: Equivalent to financial literacy for a competent adult consumer. The average item difficulty (p-value) should be 0.70–0.80.

Passing standard: 65% (≥20 of 30 questions). Item discrimination: minimum 0.20.

Question pool: Minimum 300 questions. No question reused within 90 days. Questions piloted with 50+ candidates and reviewed by two subject matter experts.

Retake policy: Unlimited; 24-hour cool-down. All attempts recorded.

Certificate validity: Lifetime. Digital credential; public registry entry.

F2 Certification — Financial Foundation Certificate

Official Title: Financial Foundation Certificate (ECFL F2)

Eligibility: Minimum Level 20; F1 certification recommended but not required.

Time limit: 90 minutes. Attempts allowed: 3 per calendar year; 7-day cool-down between attempts.

Format: 50 multiple-choice questions + 5 scenario questions (3–5 points each). Scenarios require interpretation of a brief case (max 150 words) and selection of the best course of action.

Competence area distribution: Money & Transactions 30% · Planning & Managing 30% · Risk & Reward 25% · Financial Landscape 15%.

Difficulty calibration: Equivalent to consumer financial literacy. Average item difficulty p = 0.60–0.70.

Passing standard: 70% (≥49 of 70 total points). Item discrimination: minimum 0.25.

Question pool: Minimum 400 questions; 40+ scenario items. No reuse within 180 days. Two subject matter experts per question; pilot with 75+ candidates.

Retake policy: 3 attempts per year; 7-day cool-down. All scores recorded.

Certificate validity: Lifetime (or 5-year renewal at issuer discretion). Digital credential; public registry.

F3 Certification — Financial Proficiency Certificate

Official Title: Financial Proficiency Certificate (ECFL F3)

Eligibility: Minimum Level 38; F2 certification required.

Time limit: 120 minutes. Attempts allowed: 2 per calendar year; 14-day cool-down.

Format: 50 multiple-choice questions + 8 scenario questions (5 points each) + 1 mini case study (20 points). Total: 130 points. Case study requires written justification of an investment or planning recommendation (200–400 words).

Competence area distribution: Money & Transactions 25% · Planning & Managing 25% · Risk & Reward 30% · Financial Landscape 20%.

Difficulty calibration: Equivalent to informed retail investor / junior financial professional. Average item difficulty p = 0.55–0.65.

Passing standard: 72% (≥94 of 130 points). Item discrimination: minimum 0.30.

Question pool: Minimum 500 questions; 60+ scenario items; 15+ case studies. No reuse within 270 days. Two expert reviewers per item; pilot with 100+ candidates.

Retake policy: 2 attempts per year; 14-day cool-down. All scores recorded.

Certificate validity: 3-year renewal (continuing education or exam retake). Digital credential; embossed certificate option; public registry.

F4 Certification — Financial Expertise Certificate

Official Title: Financial Expertise Certificate (ECFL F4) — Professional Level

Eligibility: Minimum Level 55; F3 certification required.

Time limit: 180 minutes. Attempts allowed: 2 per calendar year; 30-day cool-down.

Format: 50 multiple-choice questions + 10 scenario questions (6 points each) + 2 case studies (30 points each). Total: 170 points. Case studies require quantitative analysis (DCF modelling, VaR calculation, portfolio optimisation) and written interpretation.

Competence area distribution: Money & Transactions 20% · Planning & Managing 25% · Risk & Reward 35% · Financial Landscape 20%.

Difficulty calibration: Equivalent to CFA Level I. Average item difficulty p = 0.50–0.60. Tests application and analysis, not mere recognition.

Passing standard: 75% (≥128 of 170 points). Item discrimination: minimum 0.35.

Question pool: Minimum 500 questions; 60+ scenario items; 30+ case studies. No reuse within 365 days. CFA-equivalent subject matter experts; pilot with 100+ financial professionals.

Retake policy: 2 attempts per year; 30-day cool-down. All scores recorded.

Certificate validity: 3-year renewal. Prestigious digital credential; embossed certificate; public registry with professional designation.

F5 Certification — Financial Mastery Certificate

Official Title: Financial Mastery Certificate (ECFL F5) — Advanced Professional

Eligibility: Minimum Level 66; F4 certification + 3 years professional experience (verified by employer attestation or professional body membership).

Time limit: 240 minutes (separate sessions permitted). Attempts allowed: 2 per calendar year; 60-day cool-down.

Format: 40 complex multiple-choice questions + 12 scenario questions (7 points each) + 3 case studies (25 points each) + 1 research/strategy question (30 points). Total: 329 points. The research/strategy question requires a written financial strategy or analysis proposal for a complex institutional scenario (400–700 words).

Competence area distribution: Money & Transactions 15% · Planning & Managing 20% · Risk & Reward 40% · Financial Landscape 25%.

Difficulty calibration: Equivalent to CFA Level II–III. Average item difficulty p = 0.40–0.50. Tests advanced quantitative skills, strategic thinking, and integrated multi-domain judgment.

Passing standard: 78% (≥257 of 329 points). Item discrimination: minimum 0.40.

Question pool: Minimum 600 questions; 80+ case studies. No reuse within 365 days. CFA Institute-equivalent subject matter experts; pilot with 150+ senior financial professionals.

Retake policy: 2 attempts per year; 60-day cool-down. All scores recorded.

Certificate validity: 3-year renewal (continuing education or retake). Prestigious digital credential; embossed certificate; public registry.

F6 Certification — Financial Authority Certificate

Official Title: Financial Authority Certificate (ECFL F6) — Thought Leader Level

Eligibility: Minimum Level 86; F5 certification + 5 years in a senior professional role (CIO, fund manager, senior regulatory role, leading academic). Role verification required via CV, employer attestation, or publication record.

Format: Three-component evaluation, conducted over up to two calendar months:

  • Portfolio submission (50%): Candidate submits evidence of original contribution to financial knowledge, policy, or practice. Evidence may include: peer-reviewed publications, regulatory policy papers, novel strategy documentation (with performance evidence), or attested thought leadership. Portfolio is evaluated by an independent expert panel.
  • Technical examination (40%): 30 complex questions requiring deep technical knowledge and judgment + 2 strategic case studies (30 points each). Administered in 240 minutes.
  • Oral defence (10%): 30-minute structured conversation with an expert panel evaluating mastery, ability to synthesise across domains, and communication of advanced concepts to a non-specialist audience.

Competence area distribution (technical exam): Money & Transactions 10% · Planning & Managing 15% · Risk & Reward 35% · Financial Landscape 40%.

Passing standard: 80% across all three components; portfolio must demonstrate original contribution (evaluated qualitatively by panel).

Question pool: 50 curated questions (not rotated; same pool across all attempts). Expert panel reviews and updates annually.

Attempts: 1 per calendar year; 180-day cool-down between attempts. All attempts recorded with detailed panel feedback.

Certificate validity: 5-year renewal. Prestigious digital credential; embossed certificate with panellist signature line; public registry with bio and portfolio link.

3.3   Adaptive Placement Assessment

The ECFL includes an optional Adaptive Placement Assessment designed to help new learners identify their current ECFL level without requiring them to begin at Level 1. The placement assessment is not a certification and does not confer any formal credential; it is a diagnostic tool for learning pathway design.

Design: The placement assessment uses a Computer Adaptive Testing (CAT) architecture. Questions are drawn from the full 100-level bank and presented in order of increasing difficulty, adjusting after each response based on the candidate's performance. Starting difficulty is calibrated to Level 5 (early F1). The assessment continues until a stable estimate of the candidate's level is achieved, typically requiring 30–45 minutes and 40–60 questions.

Validity: The placement assessment is designed to place candidates within ±3 levels of their demonstrated competence band, as calibrated against band certification exam results across an appropriate validation sample. Placement accuracy is reviewed annually.

Use and interpretation: Placement results indicate the appropriate starting point for learning progression within the ECFL framework. Candidates may challenge their placement by attempting a higher band certification exam at any time. Placement is not binding: learners may always choose to begin at a lower level and build progressively.

3.4   Quality Assurance and Standards

The following quality principles govern all ECFL certification examinations.

  1. Item Response Theory Calibration: All certification exam questions are calibrated using Item Response Theory (IRT) to estimate item difficulty (b-parameter) and discrimination (a-parameter). Questions with discrimination below 0.20–0.30 are removed from the pool or revised.
  2. Difficulty Calibration Across Bands: Pass scores are set such that a candidate at the lower boundary of a band has approximately 50% probability of passing; a candidate at mid-band, approximately 75%; and a candidate at the top of the band, approximately 90%. This calibration ensures the certification is achievable but discriminating.
  3. Bias Review: All examination questions are reviewed by a minimum of two independent raters for language bias, cultural bias, and accessibility barriers. Items that systematically disadvantage any demographic group based on characteristics not relevant to financial competence are removed or revised.
  4. Validity Studies: Assessment bodies are expected to conduct periodic validity studies — at minimum every three years — demonstrating that ECFL band levels correlate with real-world financial decision-making outcomes, that certified candidates perform better on independent financial literacy measures, and that certification predicts professional performance where applicable.
  5. Cut Score Research: Pass scores for all band certifications are set using established standard-setting methods, including the Angoff method or contrasting-groups analysis, to ensure they reflect genuine minimum competence at the band standard rather than arbitrary percentile thresholds.

Section 5

Implementation Guidelines

5.1   For Educational Platforms and Institutions

Educational providers seeking to implement the ECFL should observe the following guidance:

  1. Map courses to ECFL bands explicitly. Every course, module, or curriculum offered under the ECFL should be assigned a target band. A course targeting F3 competences should not examine F4 material, and a course targeting F1 should not presuppose F2 knowledge. Published ECFL band mapping enables learners and employers to calibrate expectations accurately.
  2. Design assessment aligned to band can-do statements. Quiz and examination questions must test can-do descriptors from the target band. Providers must document their question-to-descriptor mapping and make it available for review by accreditation bodies.
  3. Set realistic and complete learning outcomes. An F2 certification from a provider attests to the learner having demonstrated all F2 can-do competences. Providers must not certify learners as F2-complete if significant F2 can-do areas (such as debt management or investment basics) are absent from the course.
  4. Sequence content by competence area. The EU/OECD competence area progression described in Section 4 should inform curriculum sequencing: Money and Transactions foundational skills before Risk and Reward; basic planning before advanced investment. The competence area matrix (Table 2.3) indicates relative emphasis at each band level.
  5. Use adaptive placement. Where feasible, providers should administer the ECFL Adaptive Placement Assessment to new learners before course placement, reducing misalignment between course level and learner capability.
  6. Publish ECFL band mapping publicly. Learners have the right to know which ECFL band a course is designed to deliver. Providers must make this information available on all course descriptions and marketing materials.
  7. Contribute to framework improvement. Providers are encouraged to share aggregate, anonymised data on learner performance with the ECFL Stakeholder Advisory Board to support ongoing framework calibration and validity.

5.2   For Assessment Bodies

Assessment bodies seeking ECFL accreditation to administer band certification examinations must comply with the following requirements:

  1. Adhere to exam specifications. The question counts, difficulty calibration, competence area distribution, pass scores, and question pool requirements specified in Section 3.2 are mandatory. Deviations require prior written approval from Quadratic OÜ.
  2. Maintain large, rotated question pools. Minimum pool sizes are specified per band in Section 3.2. Questions must be rotated so that no candidate encounters the same examination version in consecutive attempts. Pools must be expanded or refreshed at least every three years.
  3. Apply IRT calibration. Assessment bodies must demonstrate that question pools have been calibrated using IRT, and must maintain documentation of item difficulty and discrimination values. Questions failing discrimination thresholds must be removed from the active pool.
  4. Apply formal standard-setting methods. Pass scores must be established using recognised standard-setting methods (Angoff, contrasting groups, or equivalent). Pass scores must not be set by arbitrary percentile rank.
  5. Conduct and document bias reviews. All questions must be reviewed for language, cultural, and accessibility bias by independent reviewers with relevant diversity characteristics. Bias review documentation must be retained and made available to Quadratic OÜ on request.
  6. Share aggregate quality data. Assessment bodies must provide aggregate, anonymised data on pass rates, item performance, and candidate demographics to Quadratic OÜ annually to support framework validation and calibration.
  7. Maintain the public certificate registry. ECFL-certified candidates must be issued a unique certification number linked to a publicly verifiable digital record in the ECFL certificate registry maintained by Quadratic OÜ.

5.3   For Employers

Organisations seeking to use ECFL certifications as a framework for hiring, talent development, or compliance purposes should observe the following guidance:

  1. Understand band competence profiles. Each ECFL band is defined by a distinct profile of financial capability. F3 certifies proficiency in personal finance and basic investment; F4 certifies professional-grade technical knowledge; F5 certifies advanced mastery appropriate for senior practitioners. Employers should map role requirements to band profiles before specifying certification requirements.
  2. Apply bands as minimum standards, not absolute filters. For junior analyst or client-facing roles in financial services, F3 or F4 represents a reasonable minimum standard. For senior advisory, portfolio management, or risk management roles, F4 or F5 is appropriate. F6 is reserved for thought-leadership appointments.
  3. Use ECFL as a common language with educational partners. The ECFL provides a shared reference system for describing competence requirements to universities, training providers, and recruitment agencies. Specifying "F4-certified or equivalent" in a job description is more informative than describing a list of course names or credentials.
  4. Develop talent toward target bands. Employers are encouraged to establish ECFL progression pathways: specifying target certifications for promotion milestones, providing financial support for certification preparation, and recognising certification achievement in performance management.
  5. Account for certificate validity periods. F3–F5 certifications expire after three years. Employers should track certificate currency and require renewal or continuing education to maintain certification status for employees in roles that depend on certified competence.

5.4   For Regulators and Policymakers

Financial supervisory authorities, government ministries, and standard-setting bodies seeking to reference the ECFL in regulatory or policy instruments should observe the following guidance:

  1. Reference ECFL bands in competence requirements. Regulatory instruments specifying professional competence requirements (such as MiFID II knowledge and competence requirements for investment advisers) may reference ECFL bands as an objective, standardised measure. For example: "Investment advisers must hold a minimum F4 certification; senior advisers must maintain F5 certification."
  2. Incorporate ECFL in consumer protection frameworks. National strategies for financial literacy, consumer protection, and financial inclusion may use ECFL bands to set measurable population-level targets (e.g., "90% of adults achieving F2 or above by 2030") and to track progress over time using certification data.
  3. Support quality education provision. Regulators may encourage or require educational providers delivering financial competence curricula to map their courses to ECFL bands and to seek accreditation from ECFL-approved assessment bodies, as a quality assurance mechanism.
  4. Monitor competency trends at population level. Aggregate ECFL certification data, collected and reported by Quadratic OÜ with full privacy protections, provides a source of evidence for financial competency trends by sector, demographic group, and geography. This data can inform education policy and supervisory priority-setting.
  5. Align with international standards. Regulators are encouraged to liaise with the OECD/INFE and other international bodies to ensure ECFL adoption is consistent with evolving global financial literacy policy. Quadratic OÜ actively participates in international standard-setting dialogue.
  6. Consider public investment in access. To maximise the public benefit of the ECFL, regulators and policymakers may consider supporting free or subsidised access to placement assessment and F1–F2 certifications for adults in lower socioeconomic groups, school curricula, and financial inclusion programmes.

Appendix B

Sample Examination Questions

The following sample questions illustrate the difficulty gradient, format, and competence standards across ECFL bands. These questions are provided for guidance to assessment bodies and curriculum developers; they do not constitute part of any live question pool.

F1 Sample Question — Multiple Choice (Elementary)

"Which of the following is a basic function of money?"

a) To make people rich
b) To allow people to buy things and store value
c) To replace banks
d) To replace governments

Correct answer: b

Explanation: Money serves as a medium of exchange and store of value. Options (a), (c), and (d) reflect common misconceptions. This question tests F1 competence in the Money & Transactions area at Level 3–5 difficulty.

F2 Sample Question — Multiple Choice (Moderate)

"You have €5,000 to invest for 10 years. You are considering:
Option A: A savings account earning 1.5% per year (no risk).
Option B: A balanced mutual fund (60% bonds / 40% equities), historical average return 4.5% per year, volatility 8%.
Option C: A single stock with historical volatility 20% but average return 10% per year.

Which statement best reflects financial proficiency?"

a) Choose Option C because it has the highest return
b) Evaluate your risk tolerance and time horizon; Option B likely balances risk and return appropriately for a 10-year horizon
c) Choose Option A because it is safest
d) Choose based on the advice of friends

Correct answer: b

Explanation: F2 requires understanding of risk-return tradeoffs and time horizon effects. Option B balances growth and risk over a 10-year period. Option A is too conservative for a long horizon; Option C carries excessive concentration risk; Option D is not a rational decision process.

F3 Sample Question — Scenario-Based (Moderately Difficult)

"You are a 35-year-old earning €60,000 per year. You have €50,000 in savings. You plan to purchase a house (€300,000) in 5 years. Your mortgage will be 80% of the purchase price. You also intend to invest €200 per month in a diversified portfolio for retirement.

Scenario: Interest rates rise 1.5% over the next 2 years. House prices in your area are historically more correlated with long-term interest rates than with inflation.

What is the primary financial planning implication?"

a) You should buy the house immediately before rates rise further
b) Higher rates typically reduce house prices (improving affordability) but increase mortgage payments; adjust your savings rate to account for higher future mortgage costs
c) Higher rates have no material impact on house affordability
d) You should defer the purchase by 10 years until rates fall

Correct answer: b

Explanation: F3 requires understanding the relationship between interest rates, asset prices, and personal financial planning. A sophisticated plan recognises both the price effect (potentially improving affordability) and the mortgage cost effect (increasing ongoing payments), and adjusts savings accordingly.

F4 Sample Question — Technical Case Study

"You manage a €2M portfolio with the following allocation: 50% EU large-cap equities; 30% bonds (government and corporate mix); 15% REIT; 5% cash.

Historical correlations: equities–bonds: −0.30; equities–REIT: +0.60; bonds–REIT: −0.10.

A client requests: portfolio volatility below 10% annually; expected return at least 5% annually; quarterly rebalancing.

Current estimated portfolio volatility: 12%; expected return: 6.5%.

What is the appropriate recommendation?"

a) The portfolio already meets both goals; no changes needed
b) Increase bond allocation and reduce equity allocation to lower volatility, accepting a modest reduction in expected return; implement quarterly rebalancing
c) Sell the REIT due to its high correlation with equities
d) Move to 100% bonds to minimise risk

Correct answer: b

Explanation: F4 requires the ability to calculate portfolio statistics, understand correlation and diversification effects, and implement client-appropriate rebalancing strategies. Increasing bonds reduces volatility by exploiting the negative equity-bond correlation, while a modest return reduction is acceptable given the client's constraint of ≥5%. Option (c) is a distractor: REIT's value partly derives from its diversification from bonds; option (d) fails the return constraint.

F5 Sample Question — Advanced Strategic Analysis

"You advise a high-net-worth individual with a €10M portfolio facing a 42% marginal tax rate on capital gains. The portfolio is currently: 60% equities (significant unrealised gains); 30% bonds; 10% alternative investments. The client anticipates inheriting €5M in 3–5 years and wishes to retire in 8 years.

Scenario: Recent legislative proposals suggest capital gains tax may increase to 50% within 2 years.

Which strategy framework is most appropriate?"

a) Realise all gains immediately before tax rates increase
b) Defer all gains indefinitely to minimise current tax
c) Implement a dynamic realisation strategy: harvest losses to offset selective gains; structure succession planning using trusts or family gifting to reduce estate exposure; maintain retirement liquidity via a laddered bond and alternative portfolio; model scenarios for both tax rate outcomes
d) Liquidate equities entirely due to capital gains tax risk

Correct answer: c

Explanation: F5 requires integrating tax law, financial planning, estate planning, and scenario analysis. The sophisticated approach does not commit to either extreme (full realisation or full deferral) but uses legal structures, models uncertainty, and preserves optionality. Options (a) and (d) ignore the cost of immediate realisation; option (b) ignores liquidity needs and succession planning.

F6 Research/Strategy Assignment (Indicative Topic)

The following illustrates the type of strategic analysis question used in the F6 technical examination. Candidates are expected to produce a structured written analysis of 400–700 words demonstrating thought leadership and mastery of financial system design.

"Central banks globally are shifting from traditional monetary policy (interest rate adjustment) toward macroprudential regulation (capital requirements, loan-to-value limits, systemic risk surcharges). Simultaneously, non-bank financial institutions — private equity funds, hedge funds, and crypto-asset platforms — are growing rapidly outside traditional banking regulation.

Prepare a strategic analysis addressing:
(1) The rationale for macroprudential regulation and its advantages over conventional monetary tools;
(2) Gaps in current regulatory frameworks for non-bank financial institutions;
(3) A proposed framework addressing systemic risk without stifling financial innovation;
(4) The trade-offs between financial stability, allocative efficiency, and innovation."

Evaluation criteria:

Responses are assessed by expert panel on: (i) technical accuracy of regulatory and systemic risk concepts; (ii) quality and originality of proposed framework; (iii) sophistication of trade-off analysis; (iv) clarity of communication to a non-specialist policy audience. There is no single correct answer; responses are evaluated for depth of mastery and quality of reasoning.

Appendix C

Comparison: ECFL vs. CEFR Structure

Table C.1 provides a structured comparison of the ECFL and the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR), the framework that provided the structural model for the ECFL.

AspectCEFR (Languages)ECFL (Financial Literacy)
Number of bands 6 (A1–C2) 6 (F1–F6)
Level granularity Sub-bands (A1, A1+, A2, etc.); continuous implied scale 100 discrete numeric levels within bands; continuous scale
Target population Language learners of all ages Financial literacy learners of all ages; professionals; regulators
Descriptor approach Can-do statements for four language skills (reading, writing, listening, speaking) plus grammar and vocabulary Can-do statements for four EU/OECD competence areas (Money & Transactions, Planning & Managing, Risk & Reward, Financial Landscape)
Assessment method Standardised language tests; recognised language examinations (IELTS, Cambridge, TOEFL) Band certification examinations (F1–F6); adaptive placement assessment
Real-world anchoring Mapped to Cambridge English, IELTS, and national language proficiency certifications Mapped to PISA FinLit, EU/OECD Framework, EFPA, CFA, MiFID II competence requirements
Adoption scale 184 countries; 120M+ learners annually; embedded in national law and international policy Target: EU-wide adoption; reference standard for financial literacy policy and professional certification
Governance Council of Europe; intergovernmental body Quadratic OÜ; independent Stakeholder Advisory Board; triennial review cycle
Key advantage Cross-border equivalence; intuitive banding recognised globally Granular longitudinal measurement; financial domain specialisation; dual use (consumer and professional)
Extension beyond expert level C2 is the ceiling; no formal "above native" designation F6 explicitly designates thought leadership and original contribution, extending beyond practitioner mastery
Table C.1 — Comparison of ECFL and CEFR structural features. The ECFL draws on the CEFR's pedagogical model and can-do descriptor approach while extending it to the financial literacy domain and adding explicit professional and policy-level bands.

Note on the CEFR analogy: The ECFL adopts the CEFR's structural logic — hierarchical bands, can-do statements, continuous scale — but is not affiliated with or endorsed by the Council of Europe. The ECFL is an independent framework developed for the financial literacy domain by Quadratic OÜ.
